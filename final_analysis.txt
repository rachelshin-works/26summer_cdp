Block by Block’s system approach rests on a single design decision: use an existing, widely known consumer game(Minecraft) rather than build custom civic-design software. A base model of the site is constructed in Minecraft from pre-workshop data(GIS maps, satellite imagery, site photographs, and existing planning documents), giving residents a recognizable starting point rather than a blank canvas. Over a two-to-four day workshop, residents remodel this base in the game’s native voxel format, guided by facilitators who teach the software alongside the design brief. The theoretical method underneath this is a participatory workshop structure: pre-workshop preparation(site selection, data collection, technical setup), the workshop itself(orientation, site visit, modeling, presentation, prioritization), and a post-workshop phase where outputs are formalized into a report.
Data provenance is where the methodology gets harder to pin down. Residents generate at least two kinds of data inside the workshop: the voxel design itself, demographic details used to demonstrate inclusion(gender, age, disability status, refugee status), behavioral traces of what participants built and rebuilt, and priorities voiced during deliberation. The design output travels following logic: workshop > voxel model > expert translation into construction drawings > negotiation with local government, funders, and stakeholders > final report by Block by Block. However, the data layer doesn’t have an equally clear path. Where resident data goes after collection, who retains it, how HU-Habitat or Mojang/Microsoft use or store it, and what participants actually consented to, is not clearly addressed in the project’s public materials. Methodologically, then, Block by Block operates two parallel data pipelines with very different levels of transparency, one for design objects and the other for the people who produced it(opaque flow.)

Block by Block’s central argument is that expert-driven, top-down urban planning has historically excluded the people who use public space, and that a low-barrier, game-based tool can correct this by letting residents “speak” in a spatial language without needing design training. Its claim to being critical rests on positioning itself against conventional planning processes. It constructs itself as more inclusive, more accessible, and more legible to non-experts than a professional drafting tool would be.
The persuasive strategy operates on several registers at once. The voxel operates rhetorically. Its crudeness indicates “no expertise required” and therefore “democratic,” even though this legibility is achieved by radically simplifying the design vocabulary available to residents in the first place. Quantification does similar work. Figures like “220 minecrafters” convert participation into evidence, a number that can be cited in an annual report as proof of inclusion independent of what was actually decided or built. Also, the project’s own framing consistently foregrounds the workshop stage, the community-facing, photogenic middle of the process, while backgrounding both ends of the chain; the private platform that makes the tool possible, and the stakeholders and funders who actually ratify or veto the final design. 

For UN-Habitat, Block by Block is not a one-off experiment but a formalized method inside the Global Public Space Programme, the agency’s broader effort to produce socially inclusive, connected, and safe public space through site-specific assessment and locally grounded planning. It functions as a repeatable, exportable format that agency can point to as direct evidence of progress toward SDG 11, giving the program legitimacy value that extends well beyond any individual plaza or market it produces.
For Mojang and Microsoft, the project sits inside a different practice. A portfolio of civic-good, cause-marketing initiatives that followed Microsoft’s 2015 acquisition of Mojang, formalized through a dedicated foundation built specifically to scale this kind of program. Seen this way, Block by Block is simultaneously a genuine tool for underserved communities and a recurring proof point in Microsoft's broader narrative about Minecraft as more than a game; a piece of both online and offline infrastructure the company can point to when demonstrating the platform’s social value, in a scope of education and accessibility initiatives.

The project’s achievements are on the surface. In places like Kenya, Nepal, Block by Block created a room, a set of tools, and a formal invitation for people who are routinely excluded from planning conversations to shape a design and be heard by officials in the same room. This is not a small outcome.
However, its strength and structural condition are not separable. The workshop functions as an obligatory passage points:
1. Opaque data flow
2. Constraining design decisions to voxel box
3. Top-down decision process (eventually)

I would like to point out that Block by Block is a genuinely useful intervention operation for taking marginalized groups into some level of mainstream. But for this project to become a stronger one, it needs to treat data transparency and decision-making power with the same seriousness it currently gives to interface accessibility. Widening the range of voices included is not just a matter of equity; it is also what would actually expand the freedom of the design decisions being made, since a narrower set of participants tends to produce a narrower set of outcomes. And beyond its current dependence on a single private company's software, this model could be developed into something more open and accessible, while remaining fruitful enough to support a much wider range of projects.
